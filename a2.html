Micro Organism and Climate Change

Climate change and ocean microbes are interrelated in several ways. The impact of elevated greenhouse gas concentrations on ocean temperature, acidification, stratification, mixing, thermohaline circulation, nutrient supply, irradiation, and extreme weather events affects the marine microbiota in ways that have substantial environmental consequences, including major shifts in productivity, marine food webs, carbon export, and burial in the seabed. Marine phytoplankton performs half of the global photosynthetic CO2 fixation and half of the oxygen production, despite amounting to only ~1% of global plant biomass. Therefore, phytoplankton respond rapidly on a global scale to climate change.

Rising temperatures not only affect biological processes but also reduce water density and thereby stratification and circulation, which affect organismal dispersal and nutrient transport. Precipitation, salinity, and winds also affect stratification, mixing, and circulation. Nutrient inputs from air, river, and estuarine flows also affect microbial community composition and function, and climate change affects all these physical factors [1]. The overall relevance of microorganisms to ocean ecosystems can be appreciated from their number and biomass in the water column and subsurface: the total number of cells is more than 10^29, and the Census of Marine Life estimates that 90% of marine biomass is microbial .

Human activities have a direct impact on microorganisms and contribute to greenhouse gas emissions. Deforestation, agriculture, industrial activities, and pollution all affect microbial communities and contribute to climate change. Deforestation reduces carbon dioxide absorption by trees and affects soil microbial communities, which play a crucial role in nutrient cycling and carbon storage. Agriculture contributes to greenhouse gas emissions through the use of fertilizers, which release nitrous oxide during microbial processes, and livestock farming, which produces methane. Industrial activities, such as the burning of fossil fuels, release carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. Pollution from human activities, such as oil spills and wastewater treatment, can affect microbial communities and contribute to greenhouse gas emissions. It's crucial to understand the role of microorganisms in greenhouse gas emissions and incorporate this knowledge into research, technology development, and policy and management decisions for effective mitigation of climate change.
